# Portfolio V2.3 — Design, Animation, and Interaction Benchmark Study

**Purpose:** Give Codex a top-class creative reference set and an evidence-based design process without forcing it to copy one website or preserve the V2.2 visual language.

**Important:** These references are principles and quality bars. They are not templates.

The implementation agent must revisit each relevant source during V2.3 because websites and browser capabilities change.

---

# 1. Why the V2.2 visual direction underperformed

V2.2’s brief allowed advanced technology.

The implementation used:

- Astro;
- React;
- Motion;
- CSS;
- static SVG-like diagrams;
- hard-coded playback controls.

It did not add:

- Three.js;
- React Three Fiber;
- WebGL/WebGPU;
- GSAP;
- rich canvas visualization;
- advanced media systems;
- spatial glass;
- cinematic scene transitions.

The result was:

- technically clean;
- accessible;
- consistent;
- fast;
- visually restrained.

The issue is not that every portfolio needs 3D.

The issue is that the stated ambition—professional sci-fi, captivating, technically creative—was not explored deeply enough before the implementation direction was locked.

V2.3 must perform actual visual research and working prototypes before committing to the final language.

---

# 2. Creative benchmark principles

The final site should learn from four different disciplines.

## 2.1 Immersive creative development

Quality bar:

- interaction is part of the idea;
- motion changes how the work is understood;
- visual identity is inseparable from implementation.

## 2.2 Scientific and ML explanation

Quality bar:

- visualizations are inspectable;
- controls teach;
- the user can form a mental model;
- data and system state drive the experience.

## 2.3 Premium spatial interface design

Quality bar:

- depth and glass establish hierarchy;
- materials respond to content;
- controls feel precise;
- translucency never harms readability.

## 2.4 Editorial data storytelling

Quality bar:

- the sequence has a thesis;
- each scene advances the story;
- text and visuals cooperate;
- the user always knows why the next section matters.

V2.3 should combine all four.

# 3. Required reference set

# 3.1 Bruno Simon

**Source:** `https://bruno-simon.com/`

## Why it matters

Bruno Simon’s portfolio turns navigation into a navigable 3D world.

The 3D world is not a decorative hero background.

It is:

- the information architecture;
- the interaction model;
- the portfolio identity;
- the proof of creative-development ability.

## Principle to borrow

> The signature interaction should be the portfolio, not wallpaper behind the portfolio.

## Application to V2.3

The hero or career map should let the visitor move through real portfolio concepts:

- predictive ML;
- healthcare product;
- document intelligence;
- production agents;
- public projects.

## What not to copy

- vehicle/game navigation;
- playful low-poly world;
- exact camera behavior;
- game-style controls.

Shailesh’s version should feel more scientific, cinematic, and professional.

---

# 3.2 Lusion

**Source:** `https://lusion.co/`

**Relevant case-study source:** `https://v2.lusion.co/work/maxmara-bearing-gifts/`

## Why it matters

Lusion specializes in real-time 3D visual storytelling and documents practical performance decisions such as:

- optimized assets;
- prebaked lighting;
- device-aware implementation;
- selective real-time effects.

## Principle to borrow

> High visual ambition requires a production pipeline, not only animation code.

## Application to V2.3

Codex should treat visual assets as engineering artifacts:

- optimize geometry;
- compress textures;
- use poster images;
- lazy-load scenes;
- limit draw calls;
- test thermal behavior;
- provide standard/static tiers.

## What not to copy

- branded 3D product worlds;
- photorealistic commercial style;
- visual effects without technical meaning.

# 3.3 Active Theory

**Source:** `https://activetheory.net/`

## Why it matters

Active Theory describes its work as a blend of story, art, and technology.

That triad is the correct quality bar.

## Principle to borrow

Every major interaction should answer three questions:

1. What story does it advance?
2. What visual idea does it express?
3. What technology makes the idea possible?

If the answer is only “it looks futuristic,” remove it.

# 3.4 NASA Eyes

**Source:** `https://eyes.nasa.gov/`

## Why it matters

NASA Eyes creates a visually futuristic experience from:

- actual mission data;
- real celestial bodies;
- time navigation;
- spatial relationships;
- scientific state.

The science makes the interface feel futuristic.

The interface does not fake science to look futuristic.

## Principle to borrow

- model calibration points;

- agent state;

- research relationships;
- career chronology.

# 3.10 Apple materials and Liquid Glass

**Sources:**

- `https://developer.apple.com/design/human-interface-guidelines/materials`
- `https://developer.apple.com/documentation/technologyoverviews/liquid-glass`
- `https://developer.apple.com/videos/play/wwdc2025/219/`
- `https://developer.apple.com/videos/play/wwdc2025/356/`

## Why it matters

Apple’s guidance treats glass as a material for:

- hierarchy;
- navigation;
- controls;
- spatial separation;
- content focus.

Apple explicitly cautions against using Liquid Glass as the content layer everywhere.

## Principle to borrow

> Glass should establish an interaction layer—not reduce every surface to translucency.

## Application to V2.3

Strong uses:

- floating navigation;
- project inspector;
- system controls;
- timeline scrubber;
- research filter;
- hover/focus preview;
- command palette;
- evidence drawer;
- mobile bottom controls.

Weak uses:

- every paragraph card;
- long-form case-study body;
- data table with low contrast;
- large transparent panels over busy motion.

## Glass quality requirements

A premium glass panel should include intentional:

- blur;
- tint;
- saturation;
- inner highlight;
- edge reflection;
- subtle noise;
- background interaction;
- depth;
- shadow;
- contrast fallback.

Do not implement glass as only:

```css
background: rgba(...);
backdrop-filter: blur(...);
```

# 6. Glassmorphism specification

The owner explicitly wants more glassmorphism and modern elegant material design.

V2.3 must explore it seriously.

It must not apply it indiscriminately.

## 6.1 Glass roles

### Navigation glass

- floating;
- compact;
- morphing active state;
- high contrast;
- stable on scroll;
- mobile-safe.

### Instrument glass

For:

- agent controls;
- research filters;
- timeline scrubber;
- model controls;
- project inspector.

### Overlay glass

For:

- media details;
- evidence/source drawer;
- command palette;
- diagram zoom;
- selected node.

### Ambient glass

Rare large surfaces that create spatial separation.

## 6.2 Material hierarchy

Define tokens such as:

```text
glass-navigation
glass-instrument
glass-overlay
glass-ambient
glass-solid-fallback
```

Each needs:

- background;
- blur;
- saturation;
- border;
- inner highlight;
- shadow;
- text contrast;
- fallback.

## 6.3 Dynamic backgrounds

Test text against every animation frame or guarantee a stable backing layer.

WCAG contrast still applies.

Do not rely on automation alone for text over translucent media.

## 6.4 Reduced transparency

Support:

- forced colors;
- browsers without backdrop filter;
- reduced transparency preferences when available;
- performance fallback;
- print.

# 7. Motion language

Create one motion system.

Do not combine unrelated animation styles.

## 7.1 Suggested vocabulary

- **Assemble:** fragments join into a system.
- **Propagate:** a signal moves through a path.
- **Resolve:** uncertainty collapses into a state.
- **Escalate:** a path branches to human review.
- **Compare:** two states align and differences illuminate.
- **Zoom out:** one project becomes part of the career or research map.
- **Checkpoint:** motion pauses and preserves state.

## 7.2 Timing

Use:

- short feedback motion;
- medium interface transitions;
- long cinematic scene transitions.

Do not make every animation slow and luxurious.

Operational systems should feel responsive.

## 7.3 Page transitions

Allow:

- shared-element transitions;
- visual continuity between card and case study;
- route-level ambient change;
- project-specific color/material state.

Do not delay content for a theatrical loading screen.

## 7.4 Pointer response

Codex may explore:

- magnetic controls;
- depth response;
- subtle light tracking;
- proximity effects;
- pointer lens.

Provide touch and keyboard equivalents.

A custom cursor is permitted only when it improves state communication and never obscures the native pointer or accessibility.

# 10. Visual quality gates

A direction is not ready because it has:

- glass;
- 3D;
- motion;
- gradients.

It is ready when:

1. The hero is recognizable without the logo.
2. The first fold explains the career.
3. The interaction communicates real content.
4. Professional work remains primary.
5. Cards are understandable before hover.
6. Mobile feels intentionally designed.
7. Glass improves hierarchy.
8. Motion improves comprehension.
9. The site is usable without animation.
10. The visual identity is consistent across pages but not repetitive.
11. At least three experiences are memorable.
12. The implementation does not resemble a purchasable portfolio template.

# 12. Performance and accessibility balance

## Core principle

> Preserve access to content. Optimize the enhanced layer. Do not optimize the personality out of the site.

## Required tiers

### Enhanced

- richer 3D/motion;
- high-capability devices;
- loaded after critical content.

### Standard

- complete design;
- SVG/CSS/React;
- strong motion;
- broad support.

### Reduced

- static diagrams;
- semantic HTML;
- no lost content.

## Test

- low-end simulated CPU;
- mid-range mobile;
- WebGL off;
- reduced motion;
- no JavaScript;
- forced colors;
- 200% zoom;
- keyboard;
- screen reader.

A small performance tradeoff can be accepted when the experience creates clear value.

It must be measured and documented.

# 13. Final design instruction

Do not build a sci-fi theme.

Build a portfolio whose real technical material naturally produces a future-facing visual language.

Use:

- data;
- systems;
- models;
- state;
- projects;
- papers;
- outcomes;
- interactions.

The visual ambition should reveal the work—not replace it.
